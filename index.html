The Premise

We live in a "head-down" society, where our primary connection to the world is through a 6-inch glass screen. While technology has connected us globally, it has physically and mentally isolated us locally.

The Kinetic Cure is a journalistic investigation into the life-saving power of dance. Through the lens of traditional Kathak, modern movement, and everything in between, this project documents how students and local communities are using rhythm to fight anxiety, reclaim their physical presence, and build resilience. This isn't just about art; it's about the human struggle to stay grounded in a networked world.

Movement became my therapy when words failed. The body remembers what the mind tries to forget.

The Mission

Documenting dance.
Reporting the body.
Healing the record.

The Kinetic Cure is a multimedia research project conducted under the SANKALP guidelines at the Times School of Media. Our goal is to produce high-depth stories that explore the intersection of Society, Culture, and New Media through the medium of dance.

We believe dance is not peripheral to the mental health conversation — it is central to it. This project gives that belief an evidentiary foundation.

Feature Story · Cycle 1

When Kathak Became Confession: How Classical Dance Rewired Her Grief

She had not wept in three years. Then her guru asked her to perform Bhairavi — the raga of longing, the raga that signals the end of a concert, the raga that in classical Hindustani tradition is reserved for the hour before dusk. Something inside her finally broke open.

Meera is thirty-one, a software engineer from Delhi who began Kathak at the age of six and gave it up at twenty-two when a corporate career seemed incompatible with the discipline of a dance practice. Her mother died during the pandemic. Meera did not cry at the funeral. She did not cry for eighteen months afterwards.

"The grief became like a stone in my chest," she told me in a rehearsal room in Lajpat Nagar, her feet still moving unconsciously between sentences, the bells on her ankles a soft and constant counterpoint. "I knew it was there. I could feel its weight. But I couldn't reach it."

Her therapist suggested she return to Kathak. Not as performance. Not as accomplishment. As excavation. Within three sessions, Meera wept for the first time since her mother's death. Within eight weeks, her psychiatrist reported a statistically significant reduction in her PHQ-9 depression score.

This is not an isolated story. Across this project's first reporting cycle, we documented eight individuals for whom dance became the route to emotional recovery that cognitive behavioural therapy alone had not provided.

The interesting thing is that I didn't recommend the dance. She discovered it herself — and it did what three years of pharmacology had not.

Research Feature · Cycle 2

On Embodied Cognition: What Neuroscience Tells Us About Rhythm and the Recovering Mind

The default mode network is the brain's background hum — the introspective, self-referential circuitry that activates when we are not focused on any particular task. In depression, it tends to over-activate, looping rumination and self-criticism into a kind of internal weather that the depressed person cannot escape.

New research from IIT Bombay's neuroscience lab offers a striking finding: rhythmic, embodied movement — the kind that demands attentional presence in the body — consistently disrupts this over-activation. Among subjects who practiced structured dance for eight weeks, the functional connectivity between the default mode network and the salience network showed measurable normalisation.

"We've known for decades that exercise helps depression," Dr. Ananya Krishnan told me in her lab, surrounded by brain scans that looked like weather maps of another world. "What we didn't fully understand until recently was that rhythm is doing something distinct from general movement. The entrainment of the body to external beat appears to create a kind of neurological reset."

Rhythm is doing something distinct from general movement. Entrainment of the body to an external beat appears to create a neurological reset.

Cycle 3 asks the structural question: if dance heals, why isn't it prescribed? An investigation into institutional barriers between movement and medicine.

Investigative Feature · Cycle 3

The Prescription That Isn't: Why Dance Therapy Remains on the Margins of Indian Mental Healthcare

In the United Kingdom, dance/movement therapy is recognised as a credentialed allied health profession. In the United States, the American Dance Therapy Association has been credentialing practitioners since 1966. In India — a country with a classical dance tradition among the richest in the world, and a mental health crisis among the least resourced — dance therapy does not yet have a formal credentialing framework.

This is the central finding of Cycle 3: that the gap between the evidence (abundant) and the policy (absent) is not a scientific gap. It is a political, economic, and cultural one. Dance therapy in India is seen as supplement, not system. As art, not medicine.

"The irony is almost bitter," says Dr. Meenakshi Rao, a psychiatrist at AIIMS Delhi. "India gave this form to the world. Bharatanatyam is two thousand years old. And yet we will prescribe an SSRI before we will prescribe a Kathak class."

India gave this form to the world. Bharatanatyam is two thousand years old. And yet we will prescribe an SSRI before we will prescribe a Kathak class.

Dispatch #8: The Body Remembers What the Mind Forgets

On somatic memory, the polyvagal theory, and why trauma lives in tissue.

Bessel van der Kolk's foundational work on trauma and the body has been in circulation for decades, yet it remains poorly integrated into mainstream Indian psychiatric practice. This dispatch reports from the Odissi intensive in Bhubaneswar, where a three-day immersion was documented as a structured intervention rather than a recreational event. The findings are preliminary. The implications are not.

Dispatch #7: Against the Wellness Industrial Complex

When dance gets packaged and sold, what is lost?

There is a version of this project that could be monetised into irrelevance — turned into a subscription-based app, a certification programme, a series of motivational reels. This dispatch is an argument against that version. The radical potential of dance as therapy lies precisely in its refusal to be productised. The body does not offer returns on investment. That is its gift and its resistance.

Dispatch #6: Reading the Cognitive Revolution Through a Dancer's Eyes

Why anthropology makes an unexpected case for Kathak as humanity's oldest therapy.

The emergence of ritual dance in the archaeological record predates writing by tens of thousands of years. Dance may be the original shared fiction: a story told in synchronised bodies, an agreement to believe together, a rehearsal for the social trust on which civilisation depends.

A reporting letter from a Kolkata dance space where three women navigated depression, grief, and OCD.

Nandita, Priya, and Suchismita had not met before the first class. By the eighth week, they were each other's most consistent source of emotional support. Their therapists compared notes and found that each woman had reduced or eliminated pharmacological intervention during the same period.

Dispatch #4: What Intercept Journalism Finds That Surveys Cannot

On method, encounter, and the ethics of proximity.

The decision to use intercept interviews as a primary research method was not accidental. Surveys ask what people think. Intercepts catch what people feel in the moment after an experience. A person exiting a Bharatanatyam class at 7 PM on a Thursday is in a different emotional register than the same person filling in an online questionnaire two weeks later.
