The Premise

We live in a "head-down" society, where our primary connection to the world is through a 6-inch glass screen. While technology has connected us globally, it has physically and mentally isolated us locally.

The Kinetic Cure is a journalistic investigation into the life-saving power of dance. Through the lens of traditional Kathak, modern movement, and everything in between, this project documents how students and local communities are using rhythm to fight anxiety, reclaim their physical presence, and build resilience. This isn't just about art; it's about the human struggle to stay grounded in a networked world.

Movement became my therapy when words failed. The body remembers what the mind tries to forget.

About Me

The Heartbeat
in the Noise

My story with rhythm began at the age of three. Long before I could fully articulate my thoughts in words, I was a dancer. For nearly two decades, dance was my language, my joy, and my constant companion. But when I entered Bennett University as a first-year journalism student, that rhythm almost fell silent.

I got diagnosed with DNS — Deviated Nasal Septum — and severe allergies. The very act of breathing, which is so fundamental to life, became a struggle for me. On heavy medication and with a sudden drain on my physical stamina, I felt the weight of my life pulling me under. The simplest things — strength, endurance, daily routines — felt out of reach.

Yet, even when my body failed me, dance did not. My choreographer at Rivaaz, my dance club, stepped back into the studio — a curious, seeking space where the deadlines fall away. Waiting for music. The whole world shuts out. My breath may be laboured, but movement finds its sync perfectly.

I have learned the details; the precision of footwork, the held gestures, the facial expressions — dance has taken me far beyond the stage. Making a mistake isn't failure — it's an opportunity to correct it with grace. To remain calm in an emergency, to maintain performance despite technical glitches. The magic of human bonding — how a group of strangers can sync their bodies and souls until it's one single person.

This is not just my hobby. They saw I was different, yet returned. I knew more than just a dancer — I was an investigator. The truth I lived — relating to a dancer from Jaipur who called it their sanctuary, a real triumph, one able to continue despite everything — gives me inner peace and joy. Making me forget. I hope you feel it too.

Now, as a journalist and researcher, I am documenting not just my own story but those of others — the mental health revolution happening on dance floors, in studios, in the spaces where storytelling and movement intersect. The Kinetic Cure is that investigation made public.

Feature Story · Cycle 1

When Kathak Became Confession: How Classical Dance Rewired Her Grief

She had not wept in three years. Then her guru asked her to perform Bhairavi — the raga of longing, the raga that signals the end of a concert, the raga that in classical Hindustani tradition is reserved for the hour before dusk. Something inside her finally broke open.

Meera is thirty-one, a software engineer from Delhi who began Kathak at the age of six and gave it up at twenty-two when a corporate career seemed incompatible with the discipline of a dance practice. Her mother died during the pandemic. Meera did not cry at the funeral. She did not cry for eighteen months afterwards.

"The grief became like a stone in my chest," she told me in a rehearsal room in Lajpat Nagar, her feet still moving unconsciously between sentences, the bells on her ankles a soft and constant counterpoint. "I knew it was there. I could feel its weight. But I couldn't reach it."

Her therapist suggested she return to Kathak. Not as performance. Not as accomplishment. As excavation. Within three sessions, Meera wept for the first time since her mother's death. Within eight weeks, her psychiatrist reported a statistically significant reduction in her PHQ-9 depression score.

This is not an isolated story. Across this project's first reporting cycle, we documented eight individuals for whom dance became the route to emotional recovery that cognitive behavioural therapy alone had not provided.

The interesting thing is that I didn't recommend the dance. She discovered it herself — and it did what three years of pharmacology had not.

Research Feature · Cycle 2

On Embodied Cognition: What Neuroscience Tells Us About Rhythm and the Recovering Mind

The default mode network is the brain's background hum — the introspective, self-referential circuitry that activates when we are not focused on any particular task. In depression, it tends to over-activate, looping rumination and self-criticism into a kind of internal weather that the depressed person cannot escape.

New research from IIT Bombay's neuroscience lab offers a striking finding: rhythmic, embodied movement — the kind that demands attentional presence in the body — consistently disrupts this over-activation. Among subjects who practiced structured dance for eight weeks, the functional connectivity between the default mode network and the salience network showed measurable normalisation.

"We've known for decades that exercise helps depression," Dr. Ananya Krishnan told me in her lab, surrounded by brain scans that looked like weather maps of another world. "What we didn't fully understand until recently was that rhythm is doing something distinct from general movement. The entrainment of the body to external beat appears to create a kind of neurological reset."

Cycle 3 asks the structural question: if dance heals, why isn't it prescribed? An investigation into institutional barriers between movement and medicine.

Investigative Feature · Cycle 3

The Prescription That Isn't: Why Dance Therapy Remains on the Margins of Indian Mental Healthcare

In the United Kingdom, dance/movement therapy is recognised as a credentialed allied health profession. In the United States, the American Dance Therapy Association has been credentialing practitioners since 1966. In India — a country with a classical dance tradition among the richest in the world, and a mental health crisis among the least resourced — dance therapy does not yet have a formal credentialing framework.

This is the central finding of Cycle 3: that the gap between the evidence (abundant) and the policy (absent) is not a scientific gap. It is a political, economic, and cultural one. Dance therapy in India is seen as supplement, not system. As art, not medicine.

"The irony is almost bitter," says Dr. Meenakshi Rao, a psychiatrist at AIIMS Delhi. "India gave this form to the world. Bharatanatyam is two thousand years old. And yet we will prescribe an SSRI before we will prescribe a Kathak class."

The 3rd Year Pivot

The pressure cooker year — and the student doing the opposite

The 3rd year of a B.Tech programme is the year most students drop their hobbies "to focus." Ananya Bala is doing the exact opposite. As Secretary of Rivaaz, Bennett University's dance club, she is leading workshops, building networks, and maintaining a 16-year dance practice in the middle of one of India's most demanding engineering programmes.

By documenting her story, The Kinetic Cure is investigating the industrial management of mental health through dance — fulfilling the SANKALP requirement of exploring the political economy of culture in a tech-driven city like Noida. What follows is that conversation.

Field Portrait · Delhi, 2025
Question 01 · The 16-Year Anchor

You've been dancing since you were four. After 16 years, when you step onto the wooden floor of the Rivaaz studio, what part of your four-year-old self do you reconnect with to handle the stress of adulthood?

There's a version of me that has never grown up in that studio. She doesn't know about deadlines or CGPA or career anxiety. She just knows that when the music starts, your feet have to find the beat — and everything else falls away. When I step in, I find her again. She's the part of me that is not afraid of failing. She just tries again.

After 16 years, the practice has changed what I notice. I notice breath. I notice where I'm holding tension. I notice when I'm performing for others versus when I'm moving for myself. That kind of body awareness is what adulthood tries to strip away — and dance keeps returning it to me.

Question 02 · Why Physical Presence Matters

You have a vast network and could easily organise Zoom workshops or YouTube tutorials. Why do you insist on bringing bodies into a physical room at Bennett?

There's something that happens when 20 people hit the same beat in the same room at the same time. The floor vibrates. You can feel it through your feet before you even register it mentally. That synchrony — that physical, collective pulse — is not transmissible through a screen. You can teach the steps on YouTube. You cannot teach what it feels like to be in the room.

We don't bring dancers to Bennett to teach us how to look good on camera; we bring them to remind us how it feels to be human in a room full of other humans.

The Body's Evolution

When the body feels like it's failing you

There were months where my body genuinely couldn't do what it had always done. Stamina crashes, joint pain, the exhaustion of back-to-back academic pressure. Dance felt impossible. But I went anyway — not to perform, just to be in the space.

Even in those sessions, the rhythm did something. It gave me a container for the pain. A structure. It said: here is a beat, find it. You don't have to be excellent. Just find the beat. After 16 years, the practice becomes a relationship with your body that is more honest than almost anything else in your life. It doesn't let you pretend.

Question 04 · Screen Time & The Studio

You spend hours staring at a screen for your B.Tech. When you dance, you have to be entirely "analog." Is Rivaaz a "digital detox" for you, or is it something deeper?

It's deeper than a detox. A detox implies you're just resting from something. This is more like returning to your original language. Screens ask you to receive — to consume, to scroll, to respond. Dance asks you to produce — to generate something with your body, to commit to a movement before you can think about whether it's right. That reversal of direction is not a break. It's a restoration.

The studio is where I remember that I have a body. Eight hours of screens and you forget. You become a floating head. Rivaaz puts you back in your legs.

Question 05 · The Mudra

If you had to describe the "Kinetic Cure" in one single hand gesture or Mudra, what would it be?

It would be the Abhaya mudra — the open palm facing outward. In classical tradition, it means "fear not." But to me it also means: I am here. I am present. I am not behind a screen performing. I am here, in this room, with you. For anyone in Noida who is drowning in deadlines — that gesture is a reminder that your physical being in a room is enough.

The Collective Pulse · Field Documentation · Delhi, 2025 06
The Shift in Student Intent

From performance to survival

Q6 — As Secretary, you see students from all years. Do you notice a difference in why people join now?

It's changed visibly. In the first year, you mostly get students who want the stage — the lights, the applause, the reel footage. By 3rd year, something shifts. They come quieter. They come because they're running out of ways to cope. The academic pressure, the placement anxiety, the social isolation of a tech campus. They come to Rivaaz because it's the one hour in the week where no one is evaluating their performance against a metric.

Q7 — There's a moment in a Rivaaz rehearsal where everyone's feet hit the floor at the same time. What does that sync do?

That moment is everything. It's the reason I do this. People who came in carrying their individual weights suddenly become one organism. The anxiety that each person carried in doesn't disappear, but it stops being individual. It becomes shared. And shared weight is different. It's manageable. It's even beautiful.

Question 08 · The Future of the Niche

As someone heading into the tech industry, do you think our generation will need these "analog" dance spaces more than ever to survive the future of AI and digital isolation?

Not just more than ever — I think they'll become emergency infrastructure. When AI handles most of our productive work, what do we do with our bodies? Dance studios, classical institutes, physical workshops — they're going to be the last spaces where you can be a full person rather than a user, a consumer, or a data point. The people who will be most mentally healthy in 20 years are the ones who maintained a practice that required their physical presence. Not a subscription. A practice.

Question 09 · Dopamine vs. Discipline

Social media has condensed dance into 30-second "perfect" clips. As someone who has practised for 16 years, do you feel this "30-second culture" is stripping away the actual human substance of the art?

It's not just stripping the substance — it's inverting the purpose. Dance was always about process. The 10,000 hours. The bad days in the studio. The muscle memory built across years of repetition. When you reduce that to a 30-second reel, you're not sharing the art. You're sharing the surface of it. New students come in having watched hundreds of Reels and they think they understand Kathak. They know what it looks like. They have no idea what it feels like.

Dance studios will become emergency infrastructure — the last spaces where you can be a full person rather than a user, a consumer, or a data point.

Ananya Bala · On AI and the future of analog spaces
Question 10 · The Observer

In your 16 years, who has been your "observer"? Does having an audience that knows your struggle change the way you dance?

My mother has seen every rehearsal, every bad day, every breakthrough. She knows what a good performance costs me. When I dance for her, I'm not performing — I'm reporting. This is what the week was. This is what I worked through. That witness relationship is sacred. An audience of strangers on the internet sees the outcome. My mother sees the journey. Those are completely different forms of witnessing — and only one of them tells the truth about who you are as a dancer.

Question 11 · Real Substance

If you had to choose between a video that gets 1 million views and a 1-hour workshop that changes the mental state of just 10 students — which represents the "Real Substance" of Rivaaz?

The workshop. Without question. One million views is a number. Ten students who leave the studio lighter than they arrived — that's a consequence. That's something that echoes in their week, in their bodies, in how they sit in a lecture the next morning. No algorithm tracks that. No analytics dashboard measures it. But it's real in a way that views are not. Rivaaz exists for that room. Not for the reel.

Question 12 · Traditional vs. Workshop Culture

Do you think our generation is gravitating more towards modern workshop culture and the social media chase of views instead of traditional Kathak classes? How does that affect the mental health of young people?

Yes, and it's costing us. Workshop culture gives you a taste — a single session, a certificate, a shareable moment. Traditional institutes give you a relationship — with a guru, with a form, with your own body over time. The depth is incomparable. The mental health consequence is this: workshops give you a high. They don't build a floor. And when the semester gets brutal, when the anxiety peaks at 3am before a submission, what do you go back to? You need a floor. You need something you have built over years that holds you when everything else is collapsing. That's what a real practice is.

Rivaaz exists for that room. Not for the reel.
